Cover Letter


November 2017

Dear Members of the Peer Review Committee,

It is with great enthusiasm that I submit my peer review portfolio for your consideration. Snce beginning my role as the health educator at Bethel Elementary School in the fall of 2016, I have been working toward my initial teaching license with an endorsement in preschool through sixth grade health. Through a combination of graduate courses, workshops, and classroom work, I have sought to gain the knowledge and skills I need to improve my practice and build a health program that has a positive, meaningful, and long-term impact on my students’ well-being.

My journey to becoming a health educator actually began many years ago, when I received a Masters Degree in Education from Harvard University and subsequently taught middle school English for two years at a small, independent school in Massachusetts. I took time off from teaching to work in a community for people with disabilities, then spent a number of joyful and busy years as a stay-at-home mom to my three sons. It was my experience in parenting that ultimately led me to the field of health. One of my children struggled with significant sensory challenges and anxiety in his early years and I realized that I would need to make a serious commitment to the health of my whole family in order to support my son. I spent a great deal of time studying health topics ranging from nutrition to stress management and was delighted that when I was ready to return to work, I was able to find an education-oriented role as the Community Health Coordinator for a local health nonprofit. My two years at Health Connections provided wonderful opportunities to dig deeper into the field of health and allowed me to do everything from teaching cooking classes at elementary schools to leading smoking cessation groups for adults. The more I worked with children, the more I knew that I wanted to return to the classroom on a regular basis, where I could cultivate relationships with students and help them learn about, value, and practice good health. Needless to say, I leapt at the opportunity to teach health in Bethel and I absolutely love the work I do!

I believe passionately in the importance of providing students with health education from an early age. The skills and functional knowledge that children develop during health class can have an enormous positive impact, not simply on their childhood, but on the choices they make as adults. Ultimately, I believe that health educators can play an important role in helping children to be healthy and happy so that they can learn in school and thrive in life--what a wonderful job!

The process of working toward my license has been an arduous but powerful learning experience for me. Compiling this portfolio allowed me ample opportunities to reflect on my practice--it helped me see my strengths as well as nudge me toward improvement in my weakest areas. I consider myself a better educator because of the work I have done to earn my license and I plan to seek out more courses and workshops so that I can continue my professional growth. I am grateful for your time in reviewing my work and I look forward to hearing your feedback.

Sincerely,

Laurie Smith

As a health teacher, I especially enjoy finding creative approaches to the topics we are exploring. For example, rather than simply talking about the importance or sleep or completing a coloring page, I worked with preschoolers to build a list of good sleep habits, then gave each student a puppet, blanket, books, and other materials to practice putting a puppet to bed.

Because my space in uniquely situated in a thoroughfare that students and staff pass many times each day, I try to design engaging and educational boards that show off student work and also get people thinking. For example, when our school participated in Walk at Lunch Day, I devoted preschool-2nd grade class time to better understanding the physical benefits of walking, then had students create personalized footprints about why they like to walk. The resulting bulletin board was a somewhat zany path of footsteps that attracted quite a bit of attention from students and adults and offered a creative chance for me to promote healthy behavior.

Competency 1. The teacher uses understanding of how learners grow and develop (in cognitive, linguistic, social, emotional, and physical areas) to design and implement developmentally appropriate and challenging learning experiences.
As a preschool through sixth grade teacher, I see ample evidence of children’s developmental differences every school day. One of the fun but challenging aspects of my job is to toggle between six year-olds, who whisper, “yeah! Health time!” as I approach their classroom, and twelve year-olds, who demand to know whether class will be “fun today” when I arrive.

A constant throughout my curriculum at every grade level is significant time devoted to nutrition education. As such, nutrition is perhaps the best way for me to demonstrate my understanding of child development. While nutrition at the preschool level is very concrete with MyPlate characters and lots of play foods to touch, first and second graders enjoy trying new foods and even competing among classes to eat the most fruits and veggies. At the third and fourth grade level, students are ready for more independence and group work to explore foods but still enjoy the hands-on task of measuring sugar and need very clear directions to “connect the dots” of the lesson. Fifth and sixth graders are naturally drawn to socializing and technology, so I provide opportunities for group projects as well as independent computer research relating to nutrition. As my older students’ abstract thinking abilities develop, I challenge them with written reflections that include broad questions like, “what is food? What does it mean to be a healthy eater?” To illustrate how I approach the differing developmental needs of my students, I created a chart that highlights my approach to nutrition across the grade levels (see link below). I am also including MyPlate character cartoons that I use with early elementary students and an essential nutrients project I use with 5th and 6th graders to illustrate developmentally appropriate approaches to nutrition education.

Although I find that students are naturally interested in health because the topic is so personal, they are much more engaged, and learn more deeply, when I design my lessons with children’s developmental needs in mind. Underpinning my approach is the teacher education I received--including courses in psychology, adolescent development, risk and resilience, and learning disabilities--as both an undergraduate at Middlebury College and a graduate student at Harvard Graduate School of Education.

Competency 2. The teacher uses understanding of learners’ commonalities and individual differences within and across diverse communities to design inclusive learning experiences that enable each learner to meet high standards.
Throughout my professional life, I have had the privilege of working with diverse groups of individuals who possess many unique gifts but also face significant challenges. After college, I spent four years living and working with developmentally disabled adults in the L’Arche Tahoma Hope Community in Tacoma, Washington. This was an enormously formative experience for me and shaped how I see people--as unique and dignified individuals with much to offer and with deeply personal needs and struggles that should be respected.

At the beginning of my teaching career, I worked at the Hillside School in Marlborough Massachusetts, a private middle school for boys who have struggled to find success in traditional school settings. My students faced a variety of challenges, from learning disabilities to attachment disorders, anxiety to Asperger’s syndrome. As an English teacher, coach, and dorm parent, I sought to help my students grow by both supporting their unique needs and challenging them to improve continuously.

When I moved on to work in the field of public health at Health Connections of the Upper Valley, I had the opportunity to lead nutrition, tobacco use prevention, and cooking classes at nine local schools, which gave me the chance to work with students from a variety of backgrounds. In that role, I also worked with diverse populations as a Tobacco Treatment Specialist leading Fresh Start smoking cessation courses for adults. To support my work with people from so many age groups and backgrounds, I participated in Cultural Competency Training.

Now that I teach health, my respect has only grown for the individual gifts my students possess and challenges they face. One of the great privileges of teaching health is working with students in a field that is so deeply personal and meaningful to them. Regardless of whether my students excel in school or will never attend college, a strong foundation in health skills is relevant and important for their life. In addition to meeting regularly with classroom teachers to discuss student needs and concerns, I also work closely with paraprofessionals who remain in the classroom with students as I teach. I build lessons to ensure that multiple modes of learning are taken into account--for example, in 3/4 classes, we began by ripping paper “bacteria” and “immune cells” to simulate immune response, then moved on to a role play about the chain of infection, and spent the remainder of class in pair work brainstorming examples for each element of the infection chain. My units always involve a variety of activities to ensure that every student can access the material, develop skills, and grow in understanding.

I also make an effort to differentiate work according to my students’ needs. For example, when I expected that an assessment project about our immune system studies was too complex for a particular student, I met with his one-on-one aide ahead of time and we planned a strategy for how she could support his work. The student was able to verbally explain what he learned in the unit and, with the help of the aide, was able to copy down his responses and complete the project. He was so proud of his project that he reminded me several times to make sure I posted it on the bulletin board outside my office!

Standard 3. The teacher works with others to create environments that support individual and collaborative learning, and that encourage positive social interaction, active engagement in learning, and self-motivation.
Competency 1. The teacher collaborates with others to build a positive learning climate marked by respect, rigor, and responsibility.
My work at Health Connections of the Upper Valley provided me with the opportunity to teach in nine different schools in my region. As I led health lessons in different environments, I began to track which schools seemed most successful at engaging and supporting students and creating a positive culture for learning. A positive, supportive school climate is extremely important to me as an educator--I believe that children learn best and are able to truly thrive when we highlight their strengths, focus on the positive, and set clear expectations. Fortunately, one of the schools that most impressed me was Bethel Elementary because of their MTSS-B (formerly PBIS) program. I was thrilled when Bethel posted an opening for an elementary health educator and have truly loved working there.

I joined Bethel’s MTSS-B team at the start of the 2016-17 school year in hopes of learning about and contributing to this important program. MTSS-B at Bethel truly is “multi-tiered.” While individual classroom teachers create rewards and behavior management systems that best meet their students’ needs, all elementary teachers award paper “buzz bucks” to students whenever they exemplify the school values of respect, responsibility, and safety. The MTSS-B team also organizes monthly assemblies that focus on a “character trait of the month” and distribute awards. In addition, the team is responsible for organizing monthly “Community Builders” for the entire school, including Fall Hike Day and school spirit weeks. One Community Builder I spearheaded was the “Treats for Troops” program, which involved students donating unwanted Halloween candy to go in care packages for members of the military. Working closely with school administration and classroom teachers, I led classes across all grade levels on the effects of sugar, then followed up with a presentation about the Treats for Troops program. Students donated candy the week following Halloween and the youngest grades also created pictures and thank you notes for service members. At the end of the collection week, the nurse and I weighed the donations and I took 46 pounds of candy to a local Treats for Troops drop off station!

Within my own classroom, I utilize our “buzz buck” system by distributing special papers at the end of class where I note the student’s’ impressive behavior, whether it be great focus, helping a friend, or working through a challenging task. In addition, this year I have worked with 3rd-6th grade groups on developing a classroom culture that is Supportive, Authentic, Fun, and where Everyone contributes. Students brainstormed components of each term at the start of the year and I refer back to them frequently to build and maintain a positive culture in my classes.

Competency 2. The teacher manages the learning environment to engage learners actively
One of my favorite parts of being a teacher is finding creative ways to engage my students in meaningful topics. Although few elementary students possess a sophisticated understanding of healthy behavior, almost all are familiar with basics directives like, “eat your veggies,” “drink lots of water,” and “wear a seatbelt.” Of course, knowing the statement and practicing the behavior are two very different things! As much as possible, I try to help students dig into the “why” and “how” behind common health prescriptions. Why do we need veggies? Why does our body need so much water? How do seatbelts even work? As I design lessons, I aim to help students see topics from a different angle in hopes that what their work will resonate beyond simple health slogans. For example, instead of simply telling students to drink a lot of water, our lesson focused on how much water is actually in living things. Activities included guessing the water content of a carrot, breathing into plastic cups to observe water in our breath, drawing “watery” body parts, and coloring the quantity of water students guessed was in different bodily organs.

The lesson wrapped up with a discussion of needing to replenish all our water-filled parts by drinking water every day. Using grant funding from a WSCC grant, I was able to provide a new water bottle to every student to help them translate the lesson into the health-enhancing skill of keeping hydrated at school. This lesson was such a hit with my youngest students that I decided to take a risk and lead the exact same lesson with my 5th and 6th graders, crayons and all. The class went extremely well and I was delighted to see students across every grade level toting their new water bottles around school and keeping them at their desks. While using a single lesson for preschool through sixth grade is an exception, it was a reminder to me that thinking outside the box with lesson plans can help engage students and deepen their understanding, moving them beyond simple slogans and toward informed, healthy decision-making.

In addition to the water lesson plan just described, I am including a photo of an activity in which students “acted out” digestion with newspapers, pennies, a spray bottle and a few other tools. I am also including pictures from a seat belt safety class for first and second graders, which involved learning Newton’s law that objects in motion tend to stay in motion, practicing the theory by playing Red Light, Green light, and, finally, demonstrating how seat belts keep bodies from moving by simulating a Lego “crash” with and without seat belts in place. These photos demonstrate my commitment to teaching highly engaging health classes to maximize student learning and acquisition of skills.

Competency 2. The teacher creates learning experiences that make the discipline accessible and meaningful for learners to assure mastery of the content.
As the Community Outreach Coordinator for Health Connections, I was fortunate to have opportunities to teach students as well as lead them in school and community advocacy efforts related to tobacco prevention. At South Royalton School, I worked with the Students Against Destructive Decisions (S.A.D.D.) group to spearhead a smoking ban on the town green and playing fields. Students in the group had already attended my lessons called “Tobacco 101” and “E-Cigarettes: The Myths and the Facts.” Over the course of four months, I attended weekly S.A.D.D. meetings to help students deepen their understanding of tobacco use in public spaces and guide them through their advocacy project. It was important for the students to take ownership of the process--with me serving as a support and technical advisor--and they spent several weeks debating what the plan should entail. Students decided to create a petition for their smoking ban and gathered signatures from other students, teachers, and members of the community. We also brought the school’s fifth grade class into the process by engaging them in a green clean up activity which--to the students’ disgust--collected 788 cigarette butts in 30 minutes! I then worked with S.A.D.D. students to develop a presentation for Royalton’s Selectboard. This involved a significant time commitment from students, who had to truly master tobacco information to insure they gave a well-informed presentation and were able to answer questions. Ultimately, they created a display of the many harmful ingredients in a cigarette and a powerpoint about the effects of secondhand smoke, then invited fifth graders to bring their jars of collected cigarette butts and speak to the Selectboard about their experience cleaning the green. All the students involved were bitterly disappointed when the Selectboard narrowly defeated their proposal. It was a difficult lesson in how slowly change occurs and how advocacy efforts must be sustained. Although I transitioned jobs shortly after, South Royalton’s new VKAT group took up the issue the following year and I volunteered to continue supporting the effort. In June of 2017, armed with a fresh powerpoint and presenting to a newly-constituted board, the smoking ban passed and will soon become an ordinance in Royalton! As I reflected on this long and sometimes frustrating experience, I was enormously proud of the students for their hard work and tenacity and I also realized that the opportunity to advocate--to create a real-life solution for a community problem--had led them from basic knowledge of to proficiency in understanding tobacco issues.

Another chance I had to lead students to a deeper understanding of important issues involved working with 7th graders in Bethel on “Kick Butts Day” initiatives. Kick Butts Day is a nation-wide day of student advocacy to reduce tobacco use and draw attention to the harmful effects of tobacco. After leading tobacco education classes with students, I helped them comb through actual memos from tobacco companies that discussed marketing to youth. Students chose quotes they found alarming (such as, “young people are our best source of replacement smokers”) and copied them onto t-shirts, which we then displayed on a clothesline erected in the school’s main lobby. To personalize the effort, the seventh graders wrote “selfie statements” that proclaimed, “I am not a replacement [smoker], I am . . .” We mounted the statements around the students’ clothesline and also took photos which were uploaded to the Kick Butts Day website along with hundreds of others from students across the country. Although this effort was not as involved as my work with S.A.D.D. students, it provided an important opportunity for students to translate their knowledge into action, deepen their understanding of tobacco issues, and serve as teachers for the school and community.

Standard 5. The teacher understands how to connect concepts and use differing perspectives to engage learners in critical thinking, creativity, and collaborative problem solving related to authentic local and global issues.
Competency 1. The teacher connects concepts, perspectives from varied disciplines, and interdisciplinary themes to real world problems and issues.
One growing challenge health educators face is helping students understand the dangers of e-cigarettes. In many ways, the movement of e-cigarettes into the mainstream over the last several years parallels how traditional cigarettes came into common use decades ago--they were both branded as safe and even beneficial, promoted as cool, endorsed by celebrities, and marketed as a bold way to proclaim personal freedom. In teaching about e-cigarettes, I realized that I would need to go much deeper into issues of safety, marketing, and even how one breaks an addiction to help students truly understand this complex issue. To do this, I created a lesson that grouped students and required them to take a position on the key “pro” and “con” arguments about using e-cigarettes, then hold an actual debate in class. The debate was part science lesson, part persuasive speaking practice, and part critical thinking. While many fifth and sixth graders found the exercise challenging, especially when they had to defend a point of view they did not believe in, the debate was a powerful way to break open the topic and help students appreciate the complexity of the issues.

As much as possible in my health classes, I try to build lessons around real world issues and authentic concerns that children have. Whether we are learning about tobacco or seatbelt safety, it is important to me that students connect their health classes to their life and the world around them. Adopting a skills-based approach to health education is helpful in this regard because it allows students to translate skills not only to their lived experience but to other subjects they study at school. For example, as I developed a unit for 5th and 6th graders on accessing valid health information, I also introduced classroom teachers to the R.A.C.E. skill steps and provided them with a laminated copy of the steps to post in their room. I taught the initial skill early in the school year and provided practice for students by having them conduct internet health research about breakfast and a topic of their choosing. In addition to applying the R.A.C.E. steps in health class throughout the year, students will also be able to use them as a guide for research in every course.

For me, the skills-based approach to health education is all about making connections-- connections between content and real-life as well as connections between the different subjects at school. The skills outlined in the National Health Education Standards go much further than promoting health, they actually empower students with tools they need across disciplines and in different parts of their lives. I am still very much a novice at helping students develop skills but I am eager to refine my units and collaborate more with my peers to grow the skills-based approach across our curriculum.

Standard 6. The teacher understands and uses multiple methods of assessment to engage learners in their own growth, to monitor learner progress, and to guide the teacher’s and learner’s decision making.
Competency 1. The teacher uses designs or adapts multiple methods of assessment to document, monitor, and support learner progress appropriate for learning goals and objectives.
I use a variety of assessment techniques to help gauge what my students know and are able to do, as well as how they are progressing in their learning. Assessments are important both in helping me understand my students and reflecting upon my own teaching. I often employ simple formative assessments at the start of units, such as asking students to write out a separate sticky note for everything they know about accessing valid health information online. I collect these bits of knowledge and place them on a poster that I bring to class throughout a unit. The notes help guide my planning because they serve as an easy visual reference of what students already know (or perhaps are wrong about). They also provide a fun conclusion to our studies because students enjoy revisiting them, adding new understanding and correcting their mistakes.

In the midst of units, I rely on different formative assessments to gauge student understanding and progress. I may use exit tickets or written reflection questions to provide quick insight into what each student is learning. I also like to utilize games to assess learning. For example, when we are working on nutrition, students at every grade level seem to love food flash cards and I have designed different games that give insight into their progress on a given food topic. In addition to helping me understand the progress of each student, these mid-unit formative assessments provide me with critical feedback about how I may need to adjust my teaching to be more clear or to better challenge students.

For summative assessments at the end of units, I generally ask students to complete a project or craft, as well as a written reflection about their work (for the older grades). I am also working to incorporate performance assessments into my classes so that I can watch students actually do the skill we have studied and provide feedback. For example, at the end of a self-management unit that involved learning about the immune system and practicing illness-avoiding behaviors, students completed a project that required them to write a “care label” tag like the ones that appear on clothes to show how to care for their body. They were given materials to review their learning and had parameters to follow as well as freedom to make their work creative and funny. Many students found the project challenging because it required them to communicate in an unusual way, using brief, clear, declarative statements. Once they got the hang of the project, though, they enjoyed the process and were proud to see their work displayed on my bulletin board, which is in the main thoroughfare of the elementary school. I paired this project with a performance assessment involving proper hand washing techniques and using “glo-germ” lotion to gauge students’ success. By assigning both a project and a performance assessment, I was able to assess both what my students had learned in the unit and what they were able to do as a result of our studies.

I collect these bits of knowledge and place them on a poster that I bring to class throughout a unit. The notes help guide my planning because they serve as an easy visual reference of what students already know (or perhaps are wrong about). They also provide a fun conclusion to our studies because students enjoy revisiting them, adding new understanding and correcting their mistakes.

In the midst of units, I rely on different formative assessments to gauge student understanding and progress. I may use exit tickets or written reflection questions to provide quick insight into what each student is learning. I also like to utilize games to assess learning. For example, when we are working on nutrition, students at every grade level seem to love food flash cards and I have designed different games that give insight into their progress on a given food topic. In addition to helping me understand the progress of each student, these mid-unit formative assessments provide me with critical feedback about how I may need to adjust my teaching to be more clear or to better challenge students.

For summative assessments at the end of units, I generally ask students to complete a project or craft, as well as a written reflection about their work (for the older grades). I am also working to incorporate performance assessments into my classes so that I can watch students actually do the skill we have studied and provide feedback. For example, at the end of a self-management unit that involved learning about the immune system and practicing illness-avoiding behaviors, students completed a project that required them to write a “care label” tag like the ones that appear on clothes to show how to care for their body. They were given materials to review their learning and had parameters to follow as well as freedom to make their work creative and funny. Many students found the project challenging because it required them to communicate in an unusual way, using brief, clear, declarative statements. Once they got the hang of the project, though, they enjoyed the process and were proud to see their work displayed on my bulletin board, which is in the main thoroughfare of the elementary school. I paired this project with a performance assessment involving proper hand washing techniques and using “glo-germ” lotion to gauge students’ success. By assigning both a project and a performance assessment, I was able to assess both what my students had learned in the unit and what they were able to do as a result of our studies.

Competency 3. The teacher implements assessments in an ethical manner and minimizes bias to enable learners to display the full extent of their learning.
One of the challenges associated with meaningful student assessment is making sure it is always conducted in an ethical manner. In addition to designing formative and summative assessments that genuinely allow students to demonstrate their knowledge and skills, I believe it is also important to ensure that I assess each student fairly and honestly address any biases or blind spots I may have. For example, it is easy for expectations of a particular student to cloud a teacher’s judgement of their learning. With my students who have disabilities, I make an effort to assess them fairly regardless of whether they complete the same work as their peers or I have differentiated their assignments. My students with significant academic or behavioral challenges frequently have one-on-one aides who remain with them during health. Before I have students begin work on an assessment, I meet with the aide privately to create an assessment plan for their student. I work closely with aides to adapt assessments to insure that they reflect student learning as well as safeguard children’s dignity. With input from the aide, I modify the assessment and we also make an implementation plan to insure that we both understand how the student will be supported during class time. I try to make sure that I am also sensitive to students’ sense of self-worth in this process. For example, if I allow a student to skip a project, she may feel left out of a fun activity that her peers can do. Likewise, expecting a student with a disability to participate in a role play can be overwhelming because the assessment takes too public a form. Ultimately, I make an effort to assess my students as fairly as possible, while differentiating as needed and working closely with aides and other teachers to best meet student needs.

An element of assessment ethics that is especially important in the health is privacy. Because health class provides many opportunities to explore highly personal topics, it is important to me that students know that I respect their privacy. I work to build a respectful learning community from the beginning of the year and I make an effort to keep my commitments to students to show that I am trustworthy. I am clear with students about the sort of information that I can keep confidential (for example, feeling scared to fall asleep at night) and also the issues I am required to share with others (such as abuse in the home). Students in my classes know that they are never required to share personal information and I try to offer opportunities for them to practice honest self-assessment that they will not have to give to me. When I worked with third and fourth graders on hygiene, for example, I gave them an opportunity to take a self-assessment to judge how they are doing with personal hygiene practices. At their request, I allowed students to set up privacy screens as they wrote their answers. Because the topic made many students uncomfortable, I kept my commitment not to read their answers by giving them the choice of taking the assessments home or watching me shred them and put them in a trash (which I tied up before leaving the room). Although a few students were eager to show me their work (naturally, these were the ones with the best hygiene practices), I believe that students took the assessment more seriously and learned more from it because I made a genuine effort to respect their privacy.

Competency 2. The teacher plans instruction based on information from formative and summative assessments as well as other sources and systematically adjusts plans to meet each student’s learning needs.
I appreciate assessments both for what they tell me about student learning and what they tell me about my own teaching, as well as how they guide my lesson planning. During the video, I made notes about what students had said about digestion. Standard 8. The teacher understands and uses a variety of instructional strategies to encourage learners to develop deep understanding of content areas and their connections, and to build skills to apply knowledge in meaningful ways.
Competency 1. The teacher understands and uses a variety of instructional strategies and makes learning accessible to all learners.
Elementary health education is a field that lends itself easily to creative, engaging instructional approaches because everything from science experiments to cooking to written self-reflection fits easily within the curriculum. I design all of my units to include a variety of instructional approaches to ensure that every student can learn the skills and content, to deepen understanding, and to keep students engaged in what we are studying. In any given week’s worth of classes, I am likely to have students do a science experiment (such as dispersing oil with soap when we learn about hand washing), complete a written reflection, play a review game (like Kahoot or MyPlate Jeopardy), create and share a role play as a group, conduct internet research, or design a nutritious meal plan for several days. Teaching 4 through 12 year-olds means that I encounter all sorts of learning styles and I make an effort to mix individual and group work as well as to support different learning styles such as visual, auditory, and kinesthetic learners. Building relationships with students and understanding how they learn is also an important element of developing lessons. Because I teach several different groups at the same age level (for example, I lead 3 classes of 1st and 2nd graders each week), I also adapt instructional strategies for the specific needs of the group. For example, some of my youngest students learn best when they collaborate, while others simply goof around, so I build a group and individual work variation into my lessons to best meet the needs of each class.

One example of using multiple instructional strategies was the unit I designed for fifth and sixth graders about analyzing influences. This involved a fun activity where students tried to influence a group of peer volunteers to switch “mystery bags,” as well as studying different styles of influence, reflecting on how their personalities and values influence them, taking notes on a powerpoint about drugs and alcohol, discussing drug and alcohol use as a class, watching the video, “The Truth About Drugs,” applying their influence analysis skills to real people in the video, and designing and performing role plays about influence. The unit was quite long but allowed us to explore the skill and content in depth. Based on student assessments, I believe students made significant gains in their ability to analyze influences as well as in their understanding of how drugs, alcohol, and marijuana affect the brain. I think that including so many different instructional methods in the unit allowed for the depth of learning that took place--I believe I would have lost students very early on if each class required them to complete the same type of work throughout the unit..

A simpler example of varying instructional strategies is the safety lesson I taught preschool and kindergartners about wearing seat belts. Knowing that all of my students already knew the “right answer” that they should always wear seat belts, I decided to go deeper into the science of how seat belts work. First, we played “Red Light, Green Light,” with half the class assigned to be observers watching what happened to their classmates bodies when they had to stop for a red light. After giving all the students a chance to play the game and observe, I introduced Newton’s first law of motion and students made predictions about how it would relate to a person in a car. Using a makeshift road, a Pinewood derby car, and a Lego man, we simulated accident scenes, both with and without a seatbelt for our Lego. Once we saw that a seatbelt could, in fact, restrain the Lego man in a “crash,” we used straps to explore proper seat belt fit and the possible outcomes if a seatbelt was worn incorrectly. Although the crash simulation certainly excited my young students, I think that the physical experience of lurching forward during “Red Light, Green Light” was what really helped deepen their understanding of how seat belts work.
