Cover Letter


November 2017

Dear Members of the Peer Review Committee,

It is with great enthusiasm that I submit my peer review portfolio for your consideration. Since beginning my role as the health educator at Bethel Elementary School in the fall of 2016, I have been working toward my initial teaching license with an endorsement in preschool through sixth grade health. Through a combination of graduate courses, workshops, and classroom work, I have sought to gain the knowledge and skills I need to improve my practice and build a health program that has a positive, meaningful, and long-term impact on my students’ well-being.

My journey to becoming a health educator actually began many years ago, when I received a Masters Degree in Education from Harvard University and subsequently taught middle school English for two years at a small, independent school in Massachusetts. I took time off from teaching to work in a community for people with disabilities, then spent a number of joyful and busy years as a stay-at-home mom to my three sons. It was my experience in parenting that ultimately led me to the field of health. One of my children struggled with significant sensory challenges and anxiety in his early years and I realized that I would need to make a serious commitment to the health of my whole family in order to support my son. I spent a great deal of time studying health topics ranging from nutrition to stress management and was delighted that when I was ready to return to work, I was able to find an education-oriented role as the Community Health Coordinator for a local health nonprofit. My two years at Health Connections provided wonderful opportunities to dig deeper into the field of health and allowed me to do everything from teaching cooking classes at elementary schools to leading smoking cessation groups for adults. The more I worked with children, the more I knew that I wanted to return to the classroom on a regular basis, where I could cultivate relationships with students and help them learn about, value, and practice good health. Needless to say, I leapt at the opportunity to teach health in Bethel and I absolutely love the work I do!

I believe passionately in the importance of providing students with health education from an early age. The skills and functional knowledge that children develop during health class can have an enormous positive impact, not simply on their childhood, but on the choices they make as adults. Ultimately, I believe that health educators can play an important role in helping children to be healthy and happy so that they can learn in school and thrive in life--what a wonderful job!

The process of working toward my license has been an arduous but powerful learning experience for me. Compiling this portfolio allowed me ample opportunities to reflect on my practice--it helped me see my strengths as well as nudge me toward improvement in my weakest areas. I consider myself a better educator because of the work I have done to earn my license and I plan to seek out more courses and workshops so that I can continue my professional growth. I am grateful for your time in reviewing my work and I look forward to hearing your feedback.

Sincerely,

Laurie Smith

I also make an effort to differentiate work according to my students’ needs. For example, when I expected that an assessment project about our immune system studies was too complex for a particular student, I met with his one-on-one aide ahead of time and we planned a strategy for how she could support his work. The student was able to verbally explain what he learned in the unit and, with the help of the aide, was able to copy down his responses and complete the project. He was so proud of his project that he reminded me several times to make sure I posted it on the bulletin board outside my office!

Competency 2. The teacher manages the learning environment to engage learners actively.
One of my favorite parts of being a teacher is finding creative ways to engage my students in meaningful topics. Although few elementary students possess a sophisticated understanding of healthy behavior, almost all are familiar with basics directives like, “eat your veggies,” “drink lots of water,” and “wear a seatbelt.” Of course, knowing the statement and practicing the behavior are two very different things! As much as possible, I try to help students dig into the “why” and “how” behind common health prescriptions. Why do we need veggies? Why does our body need so much water? How do seatbelts even work? As I design lessons, I aim to help students see topics from a different angle in hopes that what their work will resonate beyond simple health slogans. For example, instead of simply telling students to drink a lot of water, our lesson focused on how much water is actually in living things. Activities included guessing the water content of a carrot, breathing into plastic cups to observe water in our breath, drawing “watery” body parts, and coloring the quantity of water students guessed was in different bodily organs.

The lesson wrapped up with a discussion of needing to replenish all our water-filled parts by drinking water every day. Using grant funding from a WSCC grant, I was able to provide a new water bottle to every student to help them translate the lesson into the health-enhancing skill of keeping hydrated at school. This lesson was such a hit with my youngest students that I decided to take a risk and lead the exact same lesson with my 5th and 6th graders, crayons and all. The class went extremely well and I was delighted to see students across every grade level toting their new water bottles around school and keeping them at their desks. While using a single lesson for preschool through sixth grade is an exception, it was a reminder to me that thinking outside the box with lesson plans can help engage students and deepen their understanding, moving them beyond simple slogans and toward informed, healthy decision-making.

In addition to the water lesson plan just described, I am including a photo of an activity in which students “acted out” digestion with newspapers, pennies, a spray bottle and a few other tools. I am also including pictures from a seat belt safety class for first and second graders, which involved learning Newton’s law that objects in motion tend to stay in motion, practicing the theory by playing Red Light, Green light, and, finally, demonstrating how seat belts keep bodies from moving by simulating a Lego “crash” with and without seat belts in place. These photos demonstrate my commitment to teaching highly engaging health classes to maximize student learning and acquisition of skills.

Competency 2. The teacher creates learning experiences that make the discipline accessible and meaningful for learners to assure mastery of the content.
As the Community Outreach Coordinator for Health Connections, I was fortunate to have opportunities to teach students as well as lead them in school and community advocacy efforts related to tobacco prevention. At South Royalton School, I worked with the Students Against Destructive Decisions (S.A.D.D.) group to spearhead a smoking ban on the town green and playing fields. Students in the group had already attended my lessons called “Tobacco 101” and “E-Cigarettes: The Myths and the Facts.” Over the course of four months, I attended weekly S.A.D.D. meetings to help students deepen their understanding of tobacco use in public spaces and guide them through their advocacy project. It was important for the students to take ownership of the process--with me serving as a support and technical advisor--and they spent several weeks debating what the plan should entail. Students decided to create a petition for their smoking ban and gathered signatures from other students, teachers, and members of the community. We also brought the school’s fifth grade class into the process by engaging them in a green clean up activity which--to the students’ disgust--collected 788 cigarette butts in 30 minutes! I then worked with S.A.D.D. students to develop a presentation for Royalton’s Selectboard. This involved a significant time commitment from students, who had to truly master tobacco information to insure they gave a well-informed presentation and were able to answer questions. Ultimately, they created a display of the many harmful ingredients in a cigarette and a powerpoint about the effects of secondhand smoke, then invited fifth graders to bring their jars of collected cigarette butts and speak to the Selectboard about their experience cleaning the green. All the students involved were bitterly disappointed when the Selectboard narrowly defeated their proposal. It was a difficult lesson in how slowly change occurs and how advocacy efforts must be sustained. Although I transitioned jobs shortly after, South Royalton’s new VKAT group took up the issue the following year and I volunteered to continue supporting the effort. In June of 2017, armed with a fresh powerpoint and presenting to a newly-constituted board, the smoking ban passed and will soon become an ordinance in Royalton! As I reflected on this long and sometimes frustrating experience, I was enormously proud of the students for their hard work and tenacity and I also realized that the opportunity to advocate--to create a real-life solution for a community problem--had led them from basic knowledge of to proficiency in understanding tobacco issues.

Another chance I had to lead students to a deeper understanding of important issues involved working with 7th graders in Bethel on “Kick Butts Day” initiatives. Kick Butts Day is a nation-wide day of student advocacy to reduce tobacco use and draw attention to the harmful effects of tobacco. After leading tobacco education classes with students, I helped them comb through actual memos from tobacco companies that discussed marketing to youth. Students chose quotes they found alarming (such as, “young people are our best source of replacement smokers”) and copied them onto t-shirts, which we then displayed on a clothesline erected in the school’s main lobby. To personalize the effort, the seventh graders wrote “selfie statements” that proclaimed, “I am not a replacement [smoker], I am . . .” We mounted the statements around the students’ clothesline and also took photos which were uploaded to the Kick Butts Day website along with hundreds of others from students across the country. Although this effort was not as involved as my work with S.A.D.D. students, it provided an important opportunity for students to translate their knowledge into action, deepen their understanding of tobacco issues, and serve as teachers for the school and community.

Standard 5. The teacher understands how to connect concepts and use differing perspectives to engage learners in critical thinking, creativity, and collaborative problem solving related to authentic local and global issues.
Competency 1. The teacher connects concepts, perspectives from varied disciplines, and interdisciplinary themes to real world problems and issues.
One growing challenge health educators face is helping students understand the dangers of e-cigarettes. In many ways, the movement of e-cigarettes into the mainstream over the last several years parallels how traditional cigarettes came into common use decades ago--they were both branded as safe and even beneficial, promoted as cool, endorsed by celebrities, and marketed as a bold way to proclaim personal freedom. In teaching about e-cigarettes, I realized that I would need to go much deeper into issues of safety, marketing, and even how one breaks an addiction to help students truly understand this complex issue. To do this, I created a lesson that grouped students and required them to take a position on the key “pro” and “con” arguments about using e-cigarettes, then hold an actual debate in class. The debate was part science lesson, part persuasive speaking practice, and part critical thinking. While many fifth and sixth graders found the exercise challenging, especially when they had to defend a point of view they did not believe in, the debate was a powerful way to break open the topic and help students appreciate the complexity of the issues.

As much as possible in my health classes, I try to build lessons around real world issues and authentic concerns that children have. Whether we are learning about tobacco or seatbelt safety, it is important to me that students connect their health classes to their life and the world around them. Adopting a skills-based approach to health education is helpful in this regard because it allows students to translate skills not only to their lived experience but to other subjects they study at school. For example, as I developed a unit for 5th and 6th graders on accessing valid health information, I also introduced classroom teachers to the R.A.C.E. skill steps and provided them with a laminated copy of the steps to post in their room. I taught the initial skill early in the school year and provided practice for students by having them conduct internet health research about breakfast and a topic of their choosing. In addition to applying the R.A.C.E. steps in health class throughout the year, students will also be able to use them as a guide for research in every course.

For me, the skills-based approach to health education is all about making connections-- connections between content and real-life as well as connections between the different subjects at school. The skills outlined in the National Health Education Standards go much further than promoting health, they actually empower students with tools they need across disciplines and in different parts of their lives. I am still very much a novice at helping students develop skills but I am eager to refine my units and collaborate more with my peers to grow the skills-based approach across our curriculum.

Standard 6. The teacher understands and uses multiple methods of assessment to engage learners in their own growth, to monitor learner progress, and to guide the teacher’s and learner’s decision making.
Competency 1. The teacher uses designs or adapts multiple methods of assessment to document, monitor, and support learner progress appropriate for learning goals and objectives.
I use a variety of assessment techniques to help gauge what my students know and are able to do, as well as how they are progressing in their learning. Assessments are important both in helping me understand my students and reflecting upon my own teaching. I often employ simple formative assessments at the start of units, such as asking students to write out a separate sticky note for everything they know about accessing valid health information online. I collect these bits of knowledge and place them on a poster that I bring to class throughout a unit. The notes help guide my planning because they serve as an easy visual reference of what students already know (or perhaps are wrong about). They also provide a fun conclusion to our studies because students enjoy revisiting them, adding new understanding and correcting their mistakes.

In the midst of units, I rely on different formative assessments to gauge student understanding and progress. I may use exit tickets or written reflection questions to provide quick insight into what each student is learning. I also like to utilize games to assess learning. For example, when we are working on nutrition, students at every grade level seem to love food flash cards and I have designed different games that give insight into their progress on a given food topic. In addition to helping me understand the progress of each student, these mid-unit formative assessments provide me with critical feedback about how I may need to adjust my teaching to be more clear or to better challenge students.

For summative assessments at the end of units, I generally ask students to complete a project or craft, as well as a written reflection about their work (for the older grades). I am also working to incorporate performance assessments into my classes so that I can watch students actually do the skill we have studied and provide feedback. For example, at the end of a self-management unit that involved learning about the immune system and practicing illness-avoiding behaviors, students completed a project that required them to write a “care label” tag like the ones that appear on clothes to show how to care for their body. They were given materials to review their learning and had parameters to follow as well as freedom to make their work creative and funny. Many students found the project challenging because it required them to communicate in an unusual way, using brief, clear, declarative statements. Once they got the hang of the project, though, they enjoyed the process and were proud to see their work displayed on my bulletin board, which is in the main thoroughfare of the elementary school. I paired this project with a performance assessment involving proper hand washing techniques and using “glo-germ” lotion to gauge students’ success. By assigning both a project and a performance assessment, I was able to assess both what my students had learned in the unit and what they were able to do as a result of our studies.

Competency 3. The teacher implements assessments in an ethical manner and minimizes bias to enable learners to display the full extent of their learning.
One of the challenges associated with meaningful student assessment is making sure it is always conducted in an ethical manner. Although the crash simulation certainly excited my young students, I think that the physical experience of lurching forward during “Red Light, Green Light” was what really helped deepen their understanding of how seat belts work.
